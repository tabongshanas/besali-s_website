1.9 Introduction on the Disappearance of a

Chief - Fouw.

When the disappearance of a chief in Besali is suspected, one of

the bekem (noble) takes upon himself to summon other nobles. It

is the responsibility of nkem Allah (head of the bekems) to perform

this duty. After the summoned, a preparation for the enthronement

of a new chief begins. Thereafter, those qualified are permitted to

see the sick chief (the grown-up princes and princesses that is

those very close relatives and friends as well as loyal citizens to

the sick leader) are allowed to go right to his private apartment

known to the people as meme-allah. From this moment onward

those who were summoned to the palace take their turn to attend

to the patient. Furthermore, those to whom the chief entrusted he

will also ensure that the heir apparent sees the critical moment of

his (sick ruler’s) life. However the heir apparent is not made to

understand that he is the one to succeed his father. Those taking

care of the patient make the public believe that the chief is sick

and is receiving medical attention.

1.10 The Final Disappearance of the Chief – Fouw

When the chief finally disappears, his subordinate or his friends

(others chieftaincy) and bekem Allah so that the necessary burial

rites could be carried. Furthermore, the corpse is guarded by

betuh and the entrance to and from the palace is strictly checked

by betuh members.

The disappearance of the chief in Mundani Besali is never

announced in the public. When he disappears the palace notables

continue to give the impression to the public that the chief is

receiving medical attention. Tuh that is guiding the corpse is given

a goat by the bereaved royal family. The goat is slaughtered,

cooked, and eaten exclusively by the Tuh guiding the corpse.

Meanwhile, the chives and Bekems in their respective houses are

equally provided with raw food a goat (for each house), salt, palm

oil, and ingredients to enable them to cook their food. These chiefs

and notables who are lodged at the palace respect Tuh. They

ensure that preparations leading to the enthronement of the new

ruler are properly carried out.

As the events unfold at the palace within this period, a series

of rites are performed both by the Bekems of the palace involved

the herbalists in the clan or village. At the memelah where the

body awaits burial, the royal cloth nchen is placed on the bare

floor and the body of the deceased ruler is placed on it. Two knots

of cowries are put into the side of his mouth, and a red hat is worn

on his head.

Then some renounced herbalists with some selected bekems

embark on fetching herbs that will be used to prepare a concoction

which the people call ellen.

These herbs are mixed alongside the blood of a goat and used to

smear the crops. Only special people have the right to grant this

herb. Part of the eloun is kept for the enthronement of a new Fouw

or successor to the throne. The particular type of eloun ensures

continuity and it is believed to give the successor the same

courage and authority as the deceased ruler. The burial of the

chiefs or notable is carried out by the chiefs and notables in the

land. It should be noted that different Mundani kingdoms exercise

slightly different burial rites. For example, in Nkong, Bechati,

Bangang, and Folipe the body of the Fouw is sat on a carefully

designed cushion chair which is kept right inside the grave with

two Ku-gang (crave status) on both sides of the corpse. This is the

manner he usually sat on his throne in the palace. While in this

position the body of the Fouw is wrapped with nchen before it is

covered with the earth.

The Ku-gang symbolizes the departed servants who will continue

to serve the departed ruler in the world of the dead. The two ku-

gangs equally represented a complete break with the past when,

instead of these ku-gangs, Fouw was buried with live slaves or

servants.

You (rank of the departed ruler), you ruled over us and your reign

was good. We are here to send you to go and rest with the other

ancestors pending your return. The gods of this land will lead you

and travel with you till you reach your destination, before your

departure you told us who will sit on your throne pending your

return. We heard you and we shall do just that. (His spray) when

you go do not forget to send us wealth and food, Bye Bye. After

this word of departure, some selected participants are empowered

to cover the body with the earth. Once the burial is over,

participants wash their hands and feet properly at lefem where

burial has to take place before leaving. A series of rites are

performed in preparation for the kingmakers in Mundani. Within

this period of preparation, the kingmakers are high in high gear,

the chiefs, notables, as well as internal and external elites of the

land equally, make preparations for the proper lodging and

boarding for the dignitaries to attend the kingmaker’s ritual. In the

case of Fouw, anybody who owns tuh in the land is invited with the

tuh. On the enthronement, one of the Bekems informs all the

princes and princesses to come to the palace early in the morning.

That morning the male and female children of the deceased ruler

are put in different rooms with the males in a separate room and

the females in another room. Bekems and those keeping the

willing will equally assemble in ntuo. There, they ensure that the

attire of the new chief to be enthroned as well as those of the

other members of his reigning council are intact and made

distinctly. More so, the sitting position of the new chief is prepared.

Nchen is used to decorate the entire sitting position of the new

chief is prepared. Nchen is used to decorate the entire sitting

place and the throne. Other chiefs and notables present are also

given seats at other sections of the ceremonial ground.

Concerning the enthronement of any chief, a throne is placed

inside a structure, which is constructed and completely covered

with nchen, leaving just an opened frontage through which others

can greet the new chiefs. A small table placed in front of his

throne is replete with royal paraphernalia such as a horsetail, a

sword, cowries, a gong, and a small calabash containing palm

wine, a leopard’s skin symbolizes the power and authority of that

ruler.

At ntuo, the children to be enthroned alongside the chief are

immediately given special attires to wear and given special seats

to seat on. The successor and members of the reigning councils of

Fouw remain seated where they are kept, while betuh moves to

the ceremonial ground proper. All the members of ta-tuh and tuh

are present at lefem and move to ceremonial ground headed by

kingmaker. Like in the enthronement of the chieftain, betuh enters

atietel with each tuh hitting rhythmically his walking stick on the

ground while the population remains silent. While entering the

arena, they provoke laughter from anyone, thus disturbing the

silence that reigns, any kingmaker nearby has the right to fines the

person. When this happens such a person pays a goat to tuh.

While at the ceremonial ground, the silence is maintained

while the betuh try to form a circle. Some masked members of ta-

tuh which are believed to possess some physical and spiritual

powers are always given. It is believed that those to carry the ta-

tuh are always smeary with special eloun that gives them powers

to shake the bells.

1.11 Enthronement proper

The enthronement proper of the new chief (Fauw) is often

regarded as the climax of Mundani enthronement process. It

usually begin in the morning immediately after the kingmaker. Two

very important rites that characterises enthronement proper of

chieftain in Mundani are the presentation of chief to the public by

bringing him out of memelah.

When total silence is regained after the kingmaker ritual,

selected members of tuh meet the other members of the new

chief’s entourage at “memelah” or at lour- “tuh”. Together, they

take the new chief to his throne at the ceremonial ground. When

the chief finally sits down, a storm of applause and ecstatic shouts

of welcome follows. Concerning the sitting position, the chief sits

with his Mafow to his left and his Nkwete to his right. Other

members of his cabinet sit at extremes. Once the ruler is seated

the people move up in turns to pay their first official respect to him

through greetings. Some go to him with gifts of kola nuts, palm

wine, goats and other gifts.

As the events continue, some of the bekem in the bereaved

land bring two palm nut cones one ripe and one unripe. They also

bring two banana bunches. These items are hung at a

conspicuous corner of the ceremonial ground. They ripe object

represents the departed ruler who is plucked from the society

while the unripe object represents the new ruler to be enthroned.

As the enthronement process also continues, a bamboo pole is

also planted deep into the ground. A cock is tied to its pointed tip

far up in the air. Marksmen of the tribe compete to cut the rope on

which the cock is tied. It is always a great honour for anyone who

cuts the rope. Some expert shooters are at times rewarded with a

traditional title after the enthronement. According to Mundani

tradition, some children are born into the royal family already with

their titles. “Ntukem” for example is the first son of the chief. The

first girl is still to be considered. A prince whom the father seems

to have a good sense of judgment and aeep knowledge of the law

of the land. He maintains law and order in the palace.

1.12 Entertainment after the First Day of

Enthronement.

On the first day of the enthronement proper, time is always

allocated to allow for the sharing and eating of the general food

and drinks known to the people as efie-lah, chiefs, notables and

other important dignitaries are taken in groups to specially

prepared rooms where they are served with foods and drinks.

Others eat and drink at atieteh (ceremonial ground).

Where the feasting is going on, the newly enthroned chief

and his reigning council remain seated during the sharing and

eating eeffinee-lah. During this time, the new ruler continues to

receive visitors who might not have had the opportunity to greet

him before. The sharing and eating eeffinee-lah generally takes

about two hours.

1.13 The Initiation Period at Lefem

Initiation at lefem begins after the kingmaker ritual. When

members of the new reigning council resume their normal

activities, the Fouw remains at the palace. He is then taken into

lefem thereafter. At lefem, the Fouw undergoes nine weeks of the

initiation process. The initiation is performed both by the

kingmakers. During the nine weeks at lefem, lefem dancing

sessions are occasionally organized to keep the Fouw entertained.

Through initiation, Fouw learns his rights and limitations, duties

and obligation as well as how to comport himself, how to speak in

public and lots of other aspects of royalty.

The initiation in this sacred shrine is at various levels. The

new Fouw is officially introduced to the women and children of his

deceased father left behind. He is introduced to those women the

palace expects more children from. He has to take them to bed as

tradition demands in order to achieve this objective. Moreover,

arrangements to marry a young girl or young girls to the new

paramount ruler during this period are made by young girls are

often made by kingmaker or had been long made by his departed

father on his behalf. The one who comes first is called

mekonghou. She has authority over her co-wives married into the

palace thereafter. At lefem, the Fouw is well fed alongside his wife

with whom he spends his nine weeks of initiation. During this

period, Fouw is smeared with special concoctions so that during

the nine weeks of initiation, his looks should command authority,

respect, and awe before his people. Notwithstanding most of these

aspects regarding enthronement in Mundani is fast disappearing.

1.14 The First Public Appearance of the

Ruler

At the end of this initiation process, Fouw made his first

public appearance. This ceremony takes a purely traditional

dimension. Invitations are dispatched to other rulers to attend this

grand occasion the first public appearance of Fouw is an occasion

whereby he is officially given the power to administer his people.

The citizens of the kingdom, other rulers, friends, and guests

attend this ceremony. At this point of departure from lefem to the

ceremonial ground, the leader of the kingmakers or any elderly

chief (all members of tuh) who participated in the initiation exercise

recite the following prayer to the congregation. His prayer is

always centered on the new paramount ruler: you were formerly

known as ‘x’ (x being the name of the new ruler), now you are

known as leopard ruler, respectable one, and superior to all, he

who catches and slaughters. You are to wear the crown of this

land. Let the gods of... (He calls the name of this land open your

eyes to see what you could not see before and your ears to hear

what you couldn’t hear before so that you can rule like the leopard

your forefathers were. Let the gods of (he calls the name of the

late be-fouw) assist you on your throne so that your reign can be

successful.

As he utters these words, his co-performers hold their two hands

together and close their mouth. When he finally utters ‘eheee’,

they all blow their palms at once and then rub their faces bringing

them down. This rite is intended to wish the ruler the blessing of

the gods as well as courage and fortitude in the discharge of his

duties.

1.15 The Actual Coming Out Of Lefem by

Fouw

Fouw is brought out of lefem after his initiation by a group called

strong kingmakers. This group consists of kingmakers and

selected chiefs. Every kingmaker in this group wears beleng and

one of the kingmakers persistently blows a trumpet. Behind the

kingmakers is a queue following a masked tuh. Fouw and his

cabinet, who are equally, dressed in belleng no dress on the upper

part of their body and no shoes on their feet, then follow them. The

selected chiefs in the group then tail the line. Women in this queue

however wear breast wear over lelleng. Every participant bends

down in such a way that the head almost touches the knee. In this

mood, they move from lefem and pass across the ceremonial

ground to ntuoh inside the palace. Their movements are very slow

and the crowd keeps total silent as this process takes place.

At ntuoh Fouw is then dressed up in his traditional regalia and

brought with an entourage to the ceremonial group. To bring him

to the ceremonial group the leader of the kingmakers’ stands

ahead assisted with three or four other kingmakers. Next to the

Fouw is his Mafow. The Fouw holds a horsetail (asangleh) in his

hand and wears ndop on his neck. His Mafow wears a hat. Behind

them is a masked tuh, His Mafow wears a hat. Behind them is a

masked tuh, some chief tail the line. Then comes the pouring of

liberation in which the gods of the land are invoked to assist the

new ruler in his rule. The word of the initiation goes thus;

From today you are given the leopard knife to carry this land

(name of “Besali”) forward as your fathers had done. The gods

that created this land will guide and protect you to succeed in your

plans. Let your people listen to you and also listen to them. This

collaboration will enable you to rule like your father and friends of

other lands. We know that you are well prepared for the challenge

that lies in front of you. We equally know that you will neither fail

your people nor your friends.

The foregoing indicates that the performance contexts of Mundani

enthronement ceremonial are a chain of events that begins with

the suspected disappearance of the chief and moves up to the

official enthronement of a new chief. Numerous rites and public

ceremonies, therefore, characterize the presence of Mundani

enthronement with the new chief bringing the center actor or the

main protagonist of the events. He undertakes a ritual journey and

is finally entrusted into the hands of the kingmakers. Apart from

Mundani enthronement rites, the performance context of the

Mundani sacred societies also has a lot of significance in the

Mundani traditional society. Apart from tuh, other sacred societies

like ku-kang, ngweh, and lefem also affect the enthronement of a

chief in Mundani in one way or the other. The Mundani chiefs

greatly need these sacred societies to enable them to reign

successfully. They also need these societies to fight against

Witchcraft practices in their land. The chief needs ngweh to glorify

and appreciate those citizens that hardly show a lot of velour in the

land. With lefem, a chieftain is able to glorify and constantly

celebrate his royal title. During enthronement, lefem keeps Fouw

entertained within the nine weeks of initiation.

1.20 Education

This is the remarkable achievements for the Fon of Besali. The

first school was opened in Besali by the Presbyterian in 1954 and

later transferred into (G.S. Abasap, G.S Abai and G.S. Ambo) a

catholic primary school and a Government secondary/high school.

He insisted that young boys and girls should go to school.

Now Besali has people with the following qualifications:

⦁ The PhDs

⦁ Many with M.A

⦁ Many with M.S and B.A

⦁ Hundreds with the ordinary and Advance levels.

Some of them are in countries like U.S.A, Germany, Switzerland

and a host of others. In short, he strives to see that everyone has

enough to eat, live well and he also follows the modern type of

education.

1.21 Agriculture

The use of iron tools has really been of great help for agriculture

to the people. If there has been a remarkable improvement in

Mundani, therefore agriculture has the highest percentage.

There are so many types of vegetables,

Fruits and variety of other crops also not forgetting animal

husbandry.

1.22 Health

Going to the Hospital is foot walk and trips sports. There was a

pandemic of Meningitis which broke out in the area and killed so

people in Besali;

Thanks to some Focolare Authorities “Dr Tim and Miss Marita”

and many others for coming in and so thinking of a health unit in

the area. For there was a distance of about 35Km to cover to

Mary Health of Africa Hospital in Fontem.

1.23 Roads

Roads are to be improved. Motor able roads have reached about

one third of the village.

Roads linking Besali:

Besali – Lebialem Division

Besali – Wabane Sub Division

Besali – Dschang

Besali – Sabes, Mamfe

The wish of the Fon is that Besali should have a park in Dschang,

Bamenda, Mamfe and Buea.

1.24 Housing

In those days, a woman was contented with one or two rooms as

a house and a store built apart but now, they aren’t sleeping

where they cook again. Their houses are now cemented, painted

and even putting of plywood.
